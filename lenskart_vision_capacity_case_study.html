01Executive Summary

The next GeoIQ problem isn't square footage: it's clinical capacity.

Lenskart has told the market exactly what FY27 looks like: a shift from a consumer-tech company to a consumer-AI company, with AI threaded through eye testing, manufacturing, store operations, and the app simultaneously, not as one flagship feature.3

At the same time, the company just completed buying out the remaining 7.65% of GeoIQ (Quantduo Technologies), taking it to 100% ownership in June 2026.1 Read together, those two facts point past the obvious next step. GeoIQ already answers "where should Lenskart open a store." The harder, more strategically central question, one Lenskart has flagged but not visibly solved, is "where should Lenskart deploy its scarce supply of eye-care capacity."

This case study proposes a Vision Capacity Intelligence Platform: the same hyperlocal modelling GeoIQ uses for site selection, redirected at optometrist allocation, remote testing rollout, and prescription level inventory, closed into a feedback loop with manufacturing.

Lenskart has built three genuinely differentiated capabilities, each publicly highlighted as strategic, each currently running as a separate system:

04The Problem

Opening a store is a one-time decision. Staffing it is a compounding one.

"GeoIQ optimizes where Lenskart deploys physical infrastructure. The next opportunity is optimizing where Lenskart deploys healthcare capacity."

Today: GeoIQ answers

Where should we open a store in Whitefield?

→

Proposed: VCI answers

What vision-care capacity should that catchment have over the next 30–90 days?

GeoIQ's hyperlocal intelligence currently stops at the door. There's limited public evidence that the same modelling is used for optometrist deployment, remote testing rollout, diagnostic equipment placement, or prescription-level inventory forecasting. As eye test volume scales toward 100 million, capacity allocation stops being a nice-to-have. It becomes the variable that determines whether a store converts a patient into a customer.

06Core Capabilities

Four capabilities, one loop

CAP 01

Vision Demand Forecasting

Predict eye test demand, appointment volume, and prescription mix at a micro-market level, not just a store-level aggregate. The same hexagonal segmentation GeoIQ already uses, pointed at clinical demand rather than footfall.6

CAP 02

Optometrist Capacity Planning

Recommend remote-optometrist allocation, staffing windows, and diagnostic-equipment placement against that forecast, routing scarce optometrists to where demand is highest, not spreading them evenly.5

CAP 03

Prescription-Aware Inventory

Forecast lens and frame demand by prescription category, not just SKU count, using GeoIQ's micro-market demographics cross-referenced with eye test outcomes and purchase history.6

CAP 04

Manufacturing Feedback Loop

Route store-level demand signals upstream into Lenskart's in-house lens and frame production planning. Bansal has explicitly said eye test data should inform product design, this makes it operational.3

Data Inputs

Every input is data Lenskart already captures, no new infrastructure required to start.

  • GeoIQ: mobility patterns, catchment clustering, demographic segmentation, travel-time analytics 7
  • Eye-care signals: test bookings, walk-in tests, remote optometry utilisation, prescription types, repeat exam rates 6
  • Commerce signals: frame and lens purchase mix, conversion rate, seasonal demand patterns
  • Store-ops signals: staffing schedules, appointment wait times, inventory movement, POS data

07AI Trust Layer

Governance is an enabling layer, not the product.

The biggest risk to AI adoption in operations isn't prediction quality. It's trust. If recommendations affecting staffing, patient appointments, and clinical workflows become a black box, adoption falls, override rates rise, and ROI disappears before Phase 2 even starts. This is especially true as Lenskart expands into AI-driven eye testing where a wrong recommendation isn't a bad frame suggestion, it's a delayed prescription.3

What a recommendation should look like

Recommended action

Increase remote optometry slots by 20% at Whitefield store, next 3 weeks

⬡ Confidence: 84% ↑ 3-week booking growth ⏱ Rising wait times 👁 +31% first-time screens

Override behaviour becomes training data, it should fall over time because confidence builds, not because no one's checking. Store managers seeing their overrides improve the model is the flywheel that earns adoption.

08AI Evaluation & Continuous Learning

Recommendations must improve. And we must know they're improving.

The value of Vision Capacity Intelligence does not come from generating recommendations, it comes from generating recommendations that consistently improve operational outcomes. Unlike traditional software, recommendation quality cannot be assumed after deployment. Forecasting models, demand signals, and AI-driven recommendations must be continuously evaluated against real-world outcomes to ensure accuracy remains aligned with business goals.

Evaluation Dimension Example Metric
Forecast Accuracy MAPE on eye test demand predictions
Recommendation Quality % of staffing recommendations accepted
Business Impact Wait-time reduction, conversion uplift
Trust Calibration Override rate vs confidence score
Model Drift Accuracy degradation across regions or seasons

Store manager overrides become particularly valuable signals. An override is not necessarily a failure, it can become labeled feedback that helps improve future recommendations and identify blind spots in the forecasting system. Over time, this creates a continuous learning loop where recommendation quality, trust, and operational outcomes are measured together rather than independently.

09Success Metrics

Beyond delivery. Beyond accuracy.

The metrics that go beyond delivery: adoption, accuracy, automation, business impact, and trust. Here's how I'd define them for this product specifically.

Dimension What It Measures
Adoption % of stores/managers actively acting on recommendations vs. ignoring them. Measured weekly, not at launch.
Accuracy Forecast error (MAPE) on eye test demand, staffing load, and prescription-category inventory. Tracked per cohort, not globally.
Automation % of restocking and scheduling decisions accepted without manual override, grows as trust compounds.
Business Impact Wait-time reduction, eye test conversion uplift, revenue per store, reduced inventory carrying cost.
Trust Override rate over time. Should fall because confidence builds, not because managers stopped reading recommendations. Confidence calibration checked quarterly.

10Rollout

Three phases. In order.

Each phase validates trust before expanding scope, because staffing recommendations that aren't trusted kill the product before manufacturing feedback loops are even on the table.

1

PILOT · 30–50 STORES · ONE CITY

Vision Demand Forecasting

Forecast eye test demand only, validated against existing manual planning. No automated action yet. Goal: establish that the model is accurate enough to trust, and that managers will look at it.

2

EXPAND · ONE REGION

Capacity Planning

Add staffing recommendations and remote-optometry allocation once forecasting trust is earned. Wait-time reduction becomes measurable here. Override rate should start declining.

3

NATIONAL · CLOSED LOOP

Manufacturing Intelligence

Connect store-level demand signals to lens and frame production planning. Completing the chain Bansal described: eye test data informing product design, demand signals reaching manufacturing in days, not weeks.3
